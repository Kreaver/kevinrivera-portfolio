About

From mechanical engineering
to machines that decide

I started in mechanical engineering at UCF because I wanted to build things that move. I'm finishing it in a robotics lab, writing the vision pipeline that tells a strawberry-harvesting robot exactly how far away a strawberry is — without a single neural network.

In August 2026 I began my M.Eng. in Robotics & Autonomy at Duke, working on a harder version of the same problem: how do you get a machine to perceive, decide, and act in the real world, reliably, with no one there to correct its mistakes?

Outside research, I build things to find out if I can — an automated chessboard that mirrors your online games with a gantry and an electromagnet, or an animatronic Stegosaurus engineered for a children's museum. Both are the same question in disguise: how do you turn an abstract decision into precise physical motion?

I've also spent the last two years running U.S. operations for a 1,000-plus member national nonprofit, and interned in cost engineering on infrastructure projects across Central Florida. I like operating problems almost as much as I like robots.

Wizardry Chess
Team Project — with Ricardo Metral & Max Eiken

A physical chessboard that plays your online games for you. A Raspberry Pi pulls live game state from the Lichess API, translates each move into X–Y coordinates, and drives a custom gantry and electromagnet to move the correct piece in real time on a laser-cut wooden board.

Built a "Loyalty Mechanic": blunder too many moves under live engine analysis, and your own pieces begin making random legal moves instead of yours.

Hurricane Wind-Load Analysis of a Roadway Stop Sign
EML 4024C, Engineering Design Practice, UCF

A coupled CFD/FEA study of a standard 30×30" MUTCD stop sign under Category 2 hurricane wind loading — fluid-dynamics simulation for drag and pressure distribution, static structural FEA for stress and deflection, and a modal analysis for vibration response.

Found the steel U-channel post, not the aluminum sign, to be the structural failure point — reaching ~124,000 psi under peak wind loading, well past yield.

Triangular Fin Heat Transfer Analysis
EML 4143, Heat Transfer II, UCF — with Camilo Reyes & Sahira Rodriguez

Modeled a triangular-profile fin under convective cooling using a 9-node finite-difference method, solving the nodal energy balance to map the temperature profile and convective heat loss along the fin.

Verified the finite-difference solution against the overall energy balance — base conduction matched total convective loss.

Truss Structural Analysis & FEA Validation
EML 4024C, Engineering Design Practice, UCF

Designed a truss structure in SolidWorks using the Weldments toolset, then validated it against hand-calculated joint forces with a static FEA study under a 60 kN external load.

Jan – Apr 2025
Smart Walker System
BME 3891, Rehabilitation Engineering, UCF — with Madison Heeney & Maliha Rahman

A modular sensor retrofit kit that turns any standard walker into a smart mobility aid. Ultrasonic proximity sensors, capacitive grip detection, and terrain/elevation sensing feed an ESP32 controller, giving users and caregivers real-time spoken alerts — "Obstacle ahead," "Caution: uneven terrain" — instead of waiting for a fall to happen.

Estimated build cost of ~$105 per unit, validated through SolidWorks simulations of mechanical strength and braking response.

May 2025 – Present
Autonomous Depth Estimation
Agricultural Robotics Lab, UCF

A low-cost depth-estimation system for a strawberry-harvesting robot's final approach — no RGB-D camera, no LiDAR, no neural network. Just two RGB cameras, deterministic color-channel processing, and a polynomial model calibrated by minimizing squared error.

0.443 mm RMS accuracy (~1% normalized error) across a 2.5–6.5 cm target range.
Currently co-authoring a research paper on this work for journal and/or conference submission — publication coming soon.
