About

Turning Ideas Into Working Systems

I began my mechanical engineering studies at UCF with a broad interest in building physical systems, without a defined specialization. Early on, I gained exposure to structured engineering workflows through research in materials science in the Dr. Sohn Research Group. There, I learned foundational lab practices—how to document experiments, prepare and test 3D-printed samples, collect and organize data, and follow repeatable procedures used in engineering research environments. This experience gave me my first understanding of basic research discipline and experimental work.

At the same time, I worked as an estimating intern in construction engineering, supporting cost analysis and infrastructure projects under predefined deliverables and established industry processes. These early roles grounded my understanding of engineering as structured, process-driven work.

A shift in direction came through a mechatronics course, where mechanical systems, electronics, and computation were treated as a unified system. For the first time, I was working on problems where system behavior emerged from the interaction of hardware and software. This is where I became interested in robotics as a discipline.

I then joined the Dr. Xu Agricultural Robotics Lab at UCF, where I work on a real-time computer vision system for distance estimation in a strawberry-harvesting robot. The project focuses on classical, non-neural methods using dual RGB cameras, along with experimental validation using custom test setups designed in SolidWorks. I also support embedded system integration and serve as a communication point with the lab lead for progress updates and technical coordination.

In parallel, I became involved in Phi Eta Mu, a fraternity that operates primarily in Spanish. At the start, I had limited fluency, so I learned Spanish throughout the initiation process in order to fully participate. Over time, I progressed into leadership roles as Secretary and later Canciller of a 40-member chapter, where I helped manage operations, maintain records, coordinate meetings, and support financial and administrative reporting. I also contributed to the development of cross-chapter events and initiatives to strengthen alumni and member engagement, including the creation of a professional networking group to support long-term connections.

Outside of lab and organizational work, I have continued to build systems through hands-on engineering projects, including an animatronic robotic system developed in collaboration with the Lawrence Hall of Science at UC Berkeley. The project integrates mechanical design, embedded control, and system-level coordination to create a fully functional interactive exhibit prototype.

Across these experiences, I have developed a more deliberate approach to engineering—starting from system behavior, breaking problems into interacting components, and iterating through design, implementation, and testing. I am now drawn to robotics because it sits at the intersection of these domains, where success depends on both technical correctness and system-level integration.

I am pursuing graduate study in Robotics & Autonomy to continue developing in this direction, working on systems where perception, computation, and actuation must all align under real-world constraints.

Wizardry Chess
Team Project — with Ricardo Metral & Max Eiken

A physical chessboard that plays your online games for you. A Raspberry Pi pulls live game state from the Lichess API, translates each move into X–Y coordinates, and drives a custom gantry and electromagnet to move the correct piece in real time on a laser-cut wooden board.

Built a "Loyalty Mechanic": blunder too many moves under live engine analysis, and your own pieces begin making random legal moves instead of yours.

Hurricane Wind-Load Analysis of a Roadway Stop Sign
EML 4024C, Engineering Design Practice, UCF

A coupled CFD/FEA study of a standard 30×30" MUTCD stop sign under Category 2 hurricane wind loading — fluid-dynamics simulation for drag and pressure distribution, static structural FEA for stress and deflection, and a modal analysis for vibration response.

Found the steel U-channel post, not the aluminum sign, to be the structural failure point — reaching ~124,000 psi under peak wind loading, well past yield.

Triangular Fin Heat Transfer Analysis
EML 4143, Heat Transfer II, UCF — with Camilo Reyes & Sahira Rodriguez

Modeled a triangular-profile fin under convective cooling using a 9-node finite-difference method, solving the nodal energy balance to map the temperature profile and convective heat loss along the fin.

Verified the finite-difference solution against the overall energy balance — base conduction matched total convective loss.

Truss Structural Analysis & FEA Validation
EML 4024C, Engineering Design Practice, UCF

Designed a truss structure in SolidWorks using the Weldments toolset, then validated it against hand-calculated joint forces with a static FEA study under a 60 kN external load.

FEA-predicted stresses and displacements matched hand calculations within a small margin, confirming the structure stays well below yield.

Smart Walker System
BME 3891, Rehabilitation Engineering, UCF — with Madison Heeney & Maliha Rahman

A modular sensor retrofit kit that turns any standard walker into a smart mobility aid. Ultrasonic proximity sensors, capacitive grip detection, and terrain/elevation sensing feed an ESP32 controller, giving users and caregivers real-time spoken alerts — "Obstacle ahead," "Caution: uneven terrain" — instead of waiting for a fall to happen.

Estimated build cost of ~$105 per unit, validated through SolidWorks simulations of mechanical strength and braking response.

May 2025 – Present
Autonomous Depth Estimation
Agricultural Robotics Lab, UCF

A low-cost depth-estimation system for a strawberry-harvesting robot's final approach — no RGB-D camera, no LiDAR, no neural network. Just two RGB cameras, deterministic color-channel processing, and a polynomial model calibrated by minimizing squared error.

0.443 mm RMS accuracy (~1% normalized error) across a 2.5–6.5 cm target range.
Currently co-authoring a research paper on this work for journal and/or conference submission — publication coming soon.
